# Navigate · Encode · Build

The condensed flywheel. Three motions, one team at a time, compounding.

## The spine

AI collapses the distance between thought and form.

A deck, a video edit, a landing page, a brief used to be the **end** of
a translation process. Now it is the **beginning** of a new loop.
Making is cheap. Knowing what to make, and whether it is any good, is
the chokepoint.

That is the shift. The response is three motions in order.

## Navigate

**Goal**: learn to work *with* the intelligence inside real work.

This is the layer most teams skip. They reach for AI as a tool for
specific tasks (write this, summarize that) without first developing
the posture that makes the rest of the loop possible.

What it looks like in practice:

- Try the work in AI before you decide whether to. Most teams under-use
  AI because they pre-decided where it would fail.
- Compare two outputs. Read what is different. The reading is the
  learning, not the output.
- Notice where AI lifts the work and where it flattens it. Different
  tasks, different bands.
- Calibrate the freedom band per step. Sometimes you want a tool;
  sometimes you want a collaborator. The judgment call is yours.

**Failure mode**: treating AI as a search engine. The answers come back
plausible-shaped, the team copy-pastes, the work flattens to the
average. Without navigation, the rest of the flywheel does not turn.

## Encode

**Goal**: turn the judgment that makes work good into substrate the
intelligence can inherit.

This is the leverage point. Once you have navigated for a while, you
notice that the same kinds of tasks come up over and over, and that
your team has a way of doing them well that nobody has written down.
Encoding is the act of writing it down in a form the intelligence can
read.

What gets encoded:

- **Rules**: the calls only seniors make.
- **Examples**: what good looks like, in concrete past work.
- **References**: brand systems, reference docs, prior good outputs.
- **Loops**: who confirms, who decides, where humans stay in the call.

The artifact is a **Skill**: one file (with optional reference files)
that hands the model your judgment.

**Failure mode**: encoding the obvious and skipping the load-bearing
parts. A Skill that says "be on-brand" is not a Skill; a Skill that
shows three on-brand examples and one off-brand miss with the diagnosis
is. Specificity is the asset.

## Build

**Goal**: turn encoded substrate into tools, workflows, and headless
capabilities.

This is where the work bends. Once a Skill exists, it can be wrapped
in any interface: a chat, a Slack bot, a CLI, an internal page, a small
app. The substrate stays; the surface bends to the work.

What this looks like at the start:

- A workflow that used to live in a chat becomes a script.
- A judgment call that used to require a senior person becomes
  available to anyone the senior person trained the Skill on.
- A long-form output (a video, a research dump) becomes the input to
  five formats, each handled by its own Skill.

What this looks like later:

- The team builds tools for itself.
- Software for few, written by the people who use it.
- The interface no longer mediates the work; the substrate does.

**Failure mode**: jumping to Build before Encode is mature. The team
ships a tool that scales the wrong workflow, then has to unbuild it.
Encode first; build only what the encoded substrate is asking for.

## How they compound

Navigate without Encode produces shallow gains that don't survive a
model change. Encode without Navigate produces Skills that don't match
how the team actually works. Build without Encode produces tools that
work for one person and break for the next.

Run the three together. The flywheel turns.

## A practical first month

- **Week 1**: pick one team, run a workshop, encode one Skill. Use it
  on real work the same day.
- **Week 2**: same team uses the Skill on three more tasks, refines it,
  adds the examples and rules that surfaced. Shares it internally.
- **Week 3**: a second team runs the same shape. Picks their own
  workflow. Ships their own Skill.
- **Week 4**: the team that started in week 1 wraps their Skill in a
  small tool. The team that started in week 2 picks up theirs.

By month two, the question shifts from "should we use AI?" to "what
should we encode next?" The flywheel is turning.
